1815

Elizabeth Heaphy was born in London to Mary Stevenson and Thomas Heaphy. Her father,Thomas, was already a painter then.

1820

Heaphy grew up in an environment that allowed her to cultivate her artistic abilities. She played with her father's anatomical figures, and began to study and practice drawing with them. When she was five years old, her mother died.

1831

Heaphy moved to Rome along with her father and her brother Thomas. She developed her painting skills here, influenced by the atmosphere of the city.Her work drew the attention of French painter Horace Vernet, who was surprised with her ability and became interested in her studies. She became known as the petite anglaise (little Englishwoman)

1842–1850

During her time in Morocco, Elizabeth became fascinated by the Moroccan lifestyle and culture. She then published her first volume of her work Sixteen Years of an Artist's Life in Morocco, Spain, and the Canary Islands in which she described her adventures. In 1846, Heaphy married Henry John Murray, a British consul in Tangier. After her marriage she began to sign her paintings as "Elizabeth Murray" instead of "E. Heaphy"

1850–1859

On their way to the Canary Islands, the Murrays passed through Andalusia,where Murray marvelled at the paintings of Bartolomé Esteban Murilloshe. She arrived in Tenerife on 23 August 1850 on the warship Hibernia. She travelled around the entire island, noting her observations for her monograph and painting both landscapes and everyday life. The first place that Murray visited was La Matanza de Acentejo, where she stayed in a house where she could see Teide, describing the town as providing perhaps the best view of the volcano than anywhere else on the island.

1849-1850

On 31 October 1849, Queen Isabella II decreed the creation of the RACBA; the Royal Academy of Fine Arts of San Miguel Arcángel, headquartered in the capital city Santa Cruz de Tenerife. Seven months later, in May 1850, Murray was named Honorary Academic of RACBA and became fully involved in the Academy's activities. She participated in all exhibitions between 1850 and 1856, and became well-recognised for her work. In her first exhibitions she showed paintings of the places she had visited such as Greece, Morocco, and Seville; later she exhibited her paintings of the Canarian landscape and the people in Tenerife. Murray introduced the watercolour technique in the Canary Islands. Her passion for architecture led her to study and paint the architecture of the Canary Islands.

1882

On December 1882, Elizabeth passed away. She lived a life with a hope for others to continue her legacy
