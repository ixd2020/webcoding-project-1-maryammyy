Step 1: Watercolours

First things first, get a good watercolour set.

Watercolors come in three different grades: children’s, student grade, and artist grade.The best watercolors for beginners are student grade. A good example is this Winson and Newton set, as it has 24 colors — which means you won’t need to mix your own colors as much. You really just need one good palette, and you’re good to go!

Step 2: Brushes

Once you’ve got your watercolor palette, it’s time to think about brushes.

Brush preferences will vary by artist, some prefer thin brushes while others like thick brushes that can cover most of the canvas easily. You can start off with a bunch of both kinds to tryout, but do remember to get brushes of quality.

Step 3:Paper Type

Watercolour Paper is your safest bet!

Watercolor requires special paper because of absorbency. Watercolor paper essentially lets the paint sit on top of the paper until it dries rather than allowing the paint to spiderweb out. If you try to paint on, say, standard 20# printer paper, the watercolor will bleed at the edges because the paper is super absorbent. (Note that you don’t have to paint on watercolor paper!)
